Chester Arthur Burnett, known to the world as Howlin' Wolf, possessed one of the most distinctive and powerful voices in American music. Standing six foot three and weighing nearly 300 pounds, he was a fearsome physical presence who matched that intensity with a howling, growling vocal style that could not be ignored. Along with Muddy Waters, he defined the Chicago blues.

Early Life

Born on June 10, 1910, in White Station, Mississippi, Chester Burnett grew up on a cotton plantation in the Delta. His father gave him a guitar at age 18, and he learned directly from Charley Patton, the "Father of the Delta Blues," who lived on a nearby plantation. He also studied harmonica with Sonny Boy Williamson II (Rice Miller). These formative lessons gave him a foundation in the rawest, most primal tradition of the blues.

Career & Music

After discovering his voice could carry across a crowded juke joint without a microphone, Howlin' Wolf honed his howling, falsetto-punctuated style through years of performing in the Mississippi Delta. His move to Chicago in 1952 put him on Chess Records alongside his great rival Muddy Waters. Where Muddy was smooth and calculated, Wolf was raw and explosive. Working with songwriter Willie Dixon, he recorded a string of blues standards through the 1950s and 1960s that became essential repertoire for rock bands on both sides of the Atlantic.

Did you know?

Howlin' Wolf was one of the few Chicago blues musicians who was financially savvy. He was the first bluesman on the South Side to own his own car, always paid his band well, and insisted on handling his own business affairs. He also went back to school in his 40s to get his GED.

Legacy

Howlin' Wolf's impact on rock music was immense. The Doors covered "Back Door Man," Led Zeppelin adapted "Killing Floor" into "The Lemon Song," and Cream recorded "Spoonful." His raw vocal style influenced everyone from Captain Beefheart to Tom Waits to Jack White. He was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1991, and "Smokestack Lightning" was named one of the greatest songs of all time by Rolling Stone. His music remains the standard for primal, uncompromising blues power.
