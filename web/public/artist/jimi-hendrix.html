Jimi Hendrix transformed the electric guitar from a musical instrument into a force of nature. In just four years of major recordings, he expanded the vocabulary of rock guitar with feedback, distortion, and wah-wah effects that no one had imagined possible. He remains the standard against which all rock guitarists are measured.

Early Life

Born in Seattle, Washington, Hendrix taught himself guitar as a teenager, playing along to blues records. After a stint in the Army, he worked as a sideman for Little Richard, the Isley Brothers, and other R&B acts before moving to New York's Greenwich Village.

Career & Music

Discovered by Chas Chandler of the Animals, Hendrix moved to London in 1966 and formed the Jimi Hendrix Experience with Noel Redding and Mitch Mitchell. Are You Experienced, Axis: Bold as Love, and Electric Ladyland redefined rock guitar. His Woodstock performance of the Star-Spangled Banner became an icon of the 1960s.

Did you know?

At the 1967 Monterey Pop Festival, Hendrix and Pete Townshend of the Who both wanted to go on before the other, fearing being upstaged. They settled it with a coin toss — the Who went first. Hendrix then set his guitar on fire, making it impossible for anyone to follow.

Legacy

He died at 27 in London. Despite recording for just four years, he is universally regarded as the greatest electric guitarist in history. His innovations in guitar tone, studio technique, and live performance continue to influence every generation of rock musicians.
