Sergei Prokofiev was one of the towering figures of 20th-century music. A virtuoso pianist and prolific composer, he created works of extraordinary range — from fierce, percussive modernism to some of the most beautiful melodies ever written. His music for ballet, film, opera, and the concert hall remains enduringly popular.

Early Life

Born in Sontsovka, Ukraine (then part of the Russian Empire), in 1891, Prokofiev was a prodigy who composed his first piano piece at age five and an opera at nine. He entered the St. Petersburg Conservatory at age 13, the youngest student in his class, where he studied with Rimsky-Korsakov and developed his reputation as a musical rebel.

Career & Music

Prokofiev's career spanned three continents and several distinct phases. His early Russian works shocked audiences with their dissonance and energy. In exile, he composed operas and ballets for Diaghilev and the Paris stage. After returning to the Soviet Union in 1936, he produced some of his most beloved works — Peter and the Wolf, the Romeo and Juliet ballet, and the Fifth Symphony — while navigating the dangerous politics of Stalinist censorship.

Did you know?

Prokofiev died on March 5, 1953, the exact same day as Joseph Stalin. Stalin's death so dominated the news that Prokofiev's passing went virtually unnoticed, and mourners could not even bring flowers to his funeral because all of Moscow's flowers had been commandeered for Stalin.

Legacy

Prokofiev left behind one of the richest catalogs of the 20th century. Peter and the Wolf introduced millions of children to orchestral music. His Romeo and Juliet ballet is performed worldwide. His piano concertos and sonatas are cornerstones of the virtuoso repertoire. His ability to write music that is simultaneously modern and deeply accessible ensures his enduring place in the concert hall.
