Felix Mendelssohn was a composer of extraordinary precocity whose elegant, luminous music bridged the Classical and Romantic eras. A brilliant pianist, conductor, and organizer, he founded the Leipzig Conservatory and conducted the historic revival of Bach's St. Matthew Passion that restored Bach to the canon of Western music.

Early Life

Born into a wealthy and cultured Jewish-German family in Hamburg, Mendelssohn was raised in Berlin with the finest education available. He began composing at an astonishing pace — by age sixteen he had written the Octet for Strings, and at seventeen the overture to A Midsummer Night's Dream, both considered masterpieces.

Career & Music

Mendelssohn's music is marked by clarity, lyricism, and impeccable craftsmanship. As conductor of the Leipzig Gewandhaus Orchestra, he championed the works of Bach, Beethoven, and Schubert. His own compositions — from the soaring Violin Concerto to the fairy-like Midsummer Night's Dream — combine Classical elegance with Romantic expressiveness. He also excelled in smaller forms, composing the beloved Songs Without Words for piano.

Did you know?

Mendelssohn's "Wedding March" from A Midsummer Night's Dream has been played at countless weddings since Queen Victoria's daughter used it at her ceremony in 1858 — eleven years after the composer's death.

Legacy

Mendelssohn's revival of Bach's music was one of the most important events in musical history. His founding of the Leipzig Conservatory shaped German musical education for generations. Though his reputation suffered unfairly from anti-Semitic attacks after his death, his Violin Concerto, symphonies, and overtures remain cornerstones of the orchestral repertoire.
