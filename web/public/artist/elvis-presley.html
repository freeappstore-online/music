Elvis Aaron Presley is the best-selling solo music artist of all time, with estimated sales of over 500 million records worldwide. He fused country, gospel, and rhythm and blues into a new sound that electrified a generation, broke racial barriers in popular music, and launched the rock and roll revolution.

Early Life

Born on January 8, 1935, in Tupelo, Mississippi, to a poor working-class family. His twin brother Jesse was stillborn. Elvis grew up singing gospel in church and was deeply influenced by the blues and country music he heard on Memphis's Beale Street after his family moved there in 1948. He received his first guitar at age 11.

Career & Music

In 1953, Elvis walked into Sun Records to record a song for his mother. Producer Sam Phillips recognized his unique ability to blend Black and white musical traditions. "That's All Right" (1954) launched his career. By 1956, he was the biggest star in America with Heartbreak Hotel, Hound Dog, and Don't Be Cruel. After two years in the Army (1958-60), he focused on Hollywood films before his legendary 1968 Comeback Special. His Las Vegas residencies in the 1970s became iconic.

Did you know?

Elvis was a natural blond. He began dyeing his hair black as a teenager because he thought it looked better. He also held an eighth-degree black belt in karate and was a lifelong collector of police badges — he once visited the White House unannounced to ask President Nixon for a Bureau of Narcotics badge.

Legacy

Elvis fundamentally changed American culture. He made rock and roll the dominant popular music form, influenced every rock artist from the Beatles to Bruce Springsteen, and remains the most commercially successful solo artist in history. Graceland is the second-most visited private home in America after the White House.
