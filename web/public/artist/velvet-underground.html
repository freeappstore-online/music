The Velvet Underground sold few records during their existence, but their influence is incalculable. Lou Reed's streetwise lyrics, John Cale's avant-garde arrangements, and their willingness to explore taboo subjects — drugs, sadomasochism, urban decay — made them the spiritual ancestors of punk, alternative, and indie rock.

Early Life

Formed in New York City in 1964 by Lou Reed and John Cale, with Sterling Morrison and Moe Tucker. Andy Warhol became their manager and patron, incorporating them into his multimedia Exploding Plastic Inevitable shows. German singer Nico featured on their debut album.

Career & Music

The Velvet Underground & Nico (1967), with its Warhol banana cover, sold poorly but became one of the most influential albums ever. White Light/White Heat (1968) was abrasive noise. The Velvet Underground (1969) was tender and acoustic. Loaded (1970) contained Rock & Roll and Sweet Jane.

Did you know?

Brian Eno said: "The first Velvet Underground album only sold 30,000 copies in its first five years, but everyone who bought one of those 30,000 copies started a band." The album cover, designed by Andy Warhol, featured a banana sticker that could be peeled off to reveal a pink banana underneath.

Legacy

Brian Eno famously said that while their first album only sold 30,000 copies, "everyone who bought one of those 30,000 copies started a band." They are the founding fathers of punk, new wave, alternative rock, indie rock, noise rock, and art rock. Reed's influence on songwriting is immeasurable.
