John Lee Hooker was a singular force in American music. His hypnotic, one-chord boogie grooves, driven by his stomping foot and droning electric guitar, sounded like nothing else in the blues. Where other bluesmen followed standard chord progressions, Hooker stayed on one chord as long as the feeling held, creating a trance-like intensity that anticipated everything from rock to funk to hip-hop.

Early Life

Born on August 22, 1912, near Clarksdale, Mississippi, John Lee Hooker was the youngest of eleven children of a sharecropping family. His stepfather, Will Moore, was a blues guitarist who played in an unusual droning, one-chord style — the style Hooker would carry forward for the rest of his life. Forbidden from playing secular music by his deeply religious mother, Hooker ran away from home at age 14, eventually making his way north through Memphis and Cincinnati.

Career & Music

Hooker's 1948 debut "Boogie Chillen'" was a sensation, and he spent the next five decades recording prolifically — over 100 albums under his own name and dozens more under pseudonyms to dodge contractual obligations. His style never changed at its core: that insistent, foot-stomping boogie driven by a single droning chord. In the late 1980s he enjoyed a massive comeback with "The Healer," collaborating with younger admirers like Bonnie Raitt, Carlos Santana, and Keith Richards who recognized him as a living link to the deepest roots of the blues.

Did you know?

John Lee Hooker recorded under so many pseudonyms to fulfill multiple record contracts simultaneously that even he lost count. Among his aliases: John Lee Booker, Johnny Lee, Texas Slim, Delta John, Birmingham Sam, and Johnny Williams. He may have recorded more songs than any blues artist in history.

Legacy

John Lee Hooker's boogie groove is one of the most recognizable sounds in popular music. The Animals, the Rolling Stones, the Doors, ZZ Top, and the White Stripes all built songs on his foundation. His "Boom Boom" became a standard, covered hundreds of times. He won four Grammy Awards and was inducted into both the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame and the Blues Hall of Fame. His influence extends beyond blues into rock, funk, and any music that values raw, repetitive groove over polished complexity.
