Robert Nesta Marley is the most iconic figure in reggae music and one of the most influential musicians in history. Through his music with the Wailers, he brought reggae from the streets of Kingston, Jamaica, to the global stage, using it as a vehicle for spiritual expression, political activism, and universal messages of love and liberation.

Early Life

Born on February 6, 1945, in Nine Mile, Saint Ann Parish, Jamaica, to a white English father and a Black Jamaican mother. He grew up in the rural Jamaican countryside before moving to Trench Town, Kingston, at age 12. In the gritty tenements of Trench Town, he met Bunny Wailer and Peter Tosh, forming the group that would change music forever.

Career & Music

The Wailers' early ska and rocksteady recordings evolved into reggae. Their first international album, Catch a Fire (1973), introduced reggae to the world. Burnin' (1973), Natty Dread (1974), Rastaman Vibration (1976), and Exodus (1977) established Marley as a global superstar. In 1976, he survived an assassination attempt two days before a peace concert in Jamaica. Exodus was named the greatest album of the 20th century by Time magazine.

Did you know?

Bob Marley was so beloved in Jamaica that when he died, he was given a state funeral attended by the Prime Minister and an estimated 300,000 mourners. He was buried in a chapel near his birthplace in Nine Mile with his red Gibson Les Paul guitar, a Bible, and a stalk of marijuana.

Legacy

Bob Marley died of melanoma on May 11, 1981, at age 36. Legend (1984), his greatest hits compilation, has sold over 30 million copies — the best-selling reggae album ever. His music remains a global symbol of resistance, peace, and Rastafarian spirituality. He was posthumously inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1994.
