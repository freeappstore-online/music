Stevland Hardaway Judkins, known as Stevie Wonder, is one of the most accomplished and celebrated musicians in history. Blind since shortly after birth, he signed with Motown at age 11 and went on to create some of the most inventive, soulful, and socially conscious music of the 20th century, pioneering the use of synthesizers and electronic instruments.

Early Life

Born on May 13, 1950, in Saginaw, Michigan. Born six weeks premature, he became blind due to retinopathy of prematurity. He showed extraordinary musical talent from a young age, mastering piano, harmonica, and drums by age nine. Ronnie White of the Miracles introduced him to Motown's Berry Gordy, who signed him as "Little Stevie Wonder" at age 11.

Career & Music

"Fingertips" (1963) made him the youngest artist to top the Billboard Hot 100 at age 13. After renegotiating his Motown contract at 21, he embarked on an unprecedented creative run: Talking Book (1972), Innervisions (1973), Fulfillingness' First Finale (1974), and Songs in the Key of Life (1976) are among the greatest albums ever made. He pioneered the use of the Moog synthesizer and TONTO in popular music.

Did you know?

Stevie Wonder's "classic period" albums won the Grammy for Album of the Year three times in four years (1974, 1975, 1977) — a feat no other artist has matched. He reportedly has over 2,000 unreleased songs in his vault.

Legacy

Stevie Wonder has won 25 Grammy Awards — the most by a solo artist. He led the campaign to make Martin Luther King Jr.'s birthday a national holiday. His music — blending soul, funk, jazz, pop, and R&B with synthesizers — influenced every genre that followed. Songs in the Key of Life consistently ranks among the greatest albums in music history.
