Nina Simone, born Eunice Kathleen Waymon, was a singular force in American music — a classically trained pianist who fused jazz, blues, folk, gospel, and classical music into something entirely her own. Known as the "High Priestess of Soul," she used her extraordinary voice and piano mastery to create music of devastating emotional power, while her fierce commitment to civil rights made her one of the movement's most important artistic voices.

Early Life

Born on February 21, 1933, in Tryon, North Carolina, Eunice Waymon was a child prodigy who began playing piano at age 3 in her mother's church. A local music teacher recognized her talent and helped fund her classical training. She studied at the Juilliard School in New York and applied to the prestigious Curtis Institute of Music in Philadelphia, but was rejected — a decision she believed was due to racial discrimination, an experience that shaped her lifelong activism. To support herself, she began playing piano in an Atlantic City bar, adopting the stage name "Nina Simone" to hide her nightclub work from her devout mother.

Career & Music

Simone's career defied categorization. Her classical training gave her a harmonic sophistication rare in popular music, while her raw emotional delivery drew from gospel, blues, and the African American experience. She could shift from tender ballads to ferocious protest songs within a single concert. Her live performances were legendary — intense, unpredictable, and deeply moving. During the 1960s, she became a central figure in the civil rights movement, writing anthems like Mississippi Goddam, Four Women, and To Be Young, Gifted and Black. Her later years in Europe saw a revival of interest in her work, and she continued performing until shortly before her death.

Did you know?

Nina Simone's dream was never to be a jazz or pop singer — she wanted to become the first Black classical concert pianist in America. She only started singing in nightclubs because the bar owner told her she had to sing as well as play piano if she wanted to keep the job.

Legacy

Nina Simone's legacy is both musical and political. She is recognized as one of the most important artists of the 20th century, an uncompromising voice who refused to separate her art from her activism. Her music has been sampled and covered by countless artists across hip-hop, pop, and electronic music. Feeling Good alone has become one of the most covered songs in history. She was posthumously inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2018 and received a Grammy Hall of Fame Award. In 2021, the Curtis Institute — which had rejected her as a student — named a building in her honor, finally acknowledging the injustice she had long spoken about.
