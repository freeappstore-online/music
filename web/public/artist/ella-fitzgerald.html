Ella Jane Fitzgerald was the most celebrated vocal artist in the history of jazz. Over a career spanning nearly six decades, her crystalline tone, three-octave range, impeccable intonation, and astonishing scat improvisation set the standard by which all jazz singers are measured. She recorded over 200 albums, won 13 Grammy Awards, and sold more than 40 million records, earning universal acclaim from audiences, critics, and fellow musicians alike.

Early Life

Born on April 25, 1917, in Newport News, Virginia, Ella Fitzgerald moved to Yonkers, New York, with her mother and stepfather as a young child. After her mother's death in 1932, she fell into a troubled period — living on the streets, running numbers for local gamblers, and spending time in a reform school. Her life changed dramatically on November 21, 1934, when she entered an amateur contest at the Apollo Theater in Harlem. She had planned to dance but, intimidated by a dance duo who preceded her, chose to sing instead. She won first place, and her career was born.

Career & Music

After Chick Webb's death in 1939, the teenage Ella led his orchestra for two years before going solo. Her partnership with impresario Norman Granz transformed her career — he paired her with the finest jazz musicians and produced the eight legendary Songbook albums (1956–1964) covering Cole Porter, the Gershwins, Rodgers and Hart, Duke Ellington, and others. These recordings are considered the definitive interpretations of the Great American Songbook. Her scat singing — using her voice as a virtuosic instrument — was unmatched, and her live performances were electrifying in their spontaneity and joy.

Did you know?

During her legendary 1960 Berlin concert, Ella forgot the lyrics to "Mack the Knife" mid-song and improvised entirely new words on the spot, scatting and joking her way through. The performance won her a Grammy and is now considered one of the greatest live recordings in jazz history.

Legacy

Ella Fitzgerald broke racial barriers throughout her career — Marilyn Monroe famously helped her secure a booking at the Mocambo nightclub in Hollywood by promising to sit in the front row every night. Her Songbook albums remain the gold standard for interpreting American popular song, and her influence extends to every genre of vocal music. She received the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the National Medal of Arts, and France's Commander of the Order of Arts and Letters. The Ella Fitzgerald Charitable Foundation continues to support music education, medical care, and community development in her name.
