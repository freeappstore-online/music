Amy Jade Winehouse was a British singer-songwriter whose deep contralto voice and brutally honest songwriting revitalized soul, jazz, and R&B for the 21st century. Her second album, Back to Black, became one of the best-selling albums in UK history and won five Grammy Awards, but her tragically short life ended at 27.

Early Life

Born on September 14, 1983, in Southgate, London, to a Jewish family with deep roots in jazz. Her grandmother was once engaged to jazz legend Ronnie Scott. She attended the Sylvia Young Theatre School and the BRIT School. By her teens, she was writing songs and performing in jazz clubs around London.

Career & Music

Her debut album Frank (2003), named after Frank Sinatra, showcased her jazz influences and won critical acclaim. Back to Black (2006) was a revelation — produced by Mark Ronson, its wall-of-sound production and Amy's raw, confessional lyrics about love, addiction, and heartbreak created something timeless. "Rehab" became a global hit and "Back to Black" a modern classic. She won five Grammy Awards in 2008 — the most by a British female artist at the time.

Did you know?

Despite her reputation for controversy, Amy was deeply studious about music. She could recite the personnel of classic jazz and soul records from memory and once corrected a journalist who misidentified a Sarah Vaughan recording. Her vocal style was directly influenced by Dinah Washington, Sarah Vaughan, and Billie Holiday.

Legacy

Amy Winehouse died of alcohol poisoning on July 23, 2011, at age 27. Despite releasing only two albums, her impact on popular music was enormous. She paved the way for Adele, Sam Smith, and countless other artists who blend retro soul with modern sensibilities. The Amy Winehouse Foundation continues her legacy, supporting young people with substance abuse issues.
