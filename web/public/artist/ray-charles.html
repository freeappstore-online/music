Ray Charles Robinson was a pioneering musician who virtually invented soul music by combining gospel, blues, jazz, and R&B. Blind since age seven, he became one of the most versatile and influential artists in American music, breaking racial and genre barriers throughout his career.

Early Life

Born on September 23, 1930, in Albany, Georgia, and raised in Greenville, Florida. He began losing his sight at age five due to glaucoma and was completely blind by seven. He attended the Florida School for the Deaf and Blind, where he learned piano, clarinet, alto saxophone, organ, and trumpet, and studied composition in braille.

Career & Music

After early recordings imitating Nat King Cole, Ray found his own voice by fusing gospel singing with secular lyrics — scandalous at the time. "I Got a Woman" (1954) is widely considered the first soul song. "What'd I Say" (1959) became a crossover smash. He then shocked the music world by recording a country album, Modern Sounds in Country and Western Music (1962), which became a massive hit and broke racial barriers in country music.

Did you know?

Frank Sinatra called Ray Charles "the only true genius in show business." Ray's recording of "America the Beautiful" has become so iconic that there have been multiple campaigns to make it the national anthem. He could also repair his own electronic equipment despite being blind.

Legacy

Ray Charles won 17 Grammy Awards and was among the first inductees into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1986). Frank Sinatra called him "the only true genius in show business." His music influenced every genre of American popular music. Georgia on My Mind was adopted as the official state song of Georgia in 1979.
