Aretha Louise Franklin is universally regarded as the greatest female vocalist of all time. Her extraordinary voice — spanning four octaves with unmatched power, emotion, and gospel fervor — transformed popular music and became a soundtrack for the civil rights and feminist movements of the 1960s and 1970s.

Early Life

Born on March 25, 1942, in Memphis, Tennessee, and raised in Detroit. The daughter of famous Baptist minister C.L. Franklin, she grew up surrounded by gospel music and visiting luminaries like Martin Luther King Jr. and Sam Cooke. She began singing in her father's church at age 10 and was a mother by 14. She signed with Columbia Records at 18.

Career & Music

After six years at Columbia with limited success, Aretha moved to Atlantic Records in 1966. Producer Jerry Wexler took her to Muscle Shoals, Alabama, where she recorded her first Atlantic single — "I Never Loved a Man (The Way I Love You)" — followed immediately by "Respect," which became the defining song of a generation. Through the late 1960s and 1970s, she released a string of landmark albums blending soul, gospel, R&B, and pop.

Did you know?

Aretha's purse became legendary. She insisted on being paid in cash before every performance — and kept the money in her handbag, which she placed on the piano onstage where she could see it at all times during the show.

Legacy

Aretha Franklin won 18 Grammy Awards and was the first woman inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame (1987). Her version of "Respect" is considered the greatest song of all time by Rolling Stone. She sang at the inaugurations of three presidents. Her voice remains the benchmark against which all female vocalists are measured.
