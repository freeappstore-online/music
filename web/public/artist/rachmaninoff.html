Sergei Rachmaninoff was a towering figure of early 20th-century music — equally celebrated as a composer, pianist, and conductor. His sweeping melodies, lush harmonies, and formidable virtuosity made him the last great voice of Russian Romanticism. His piano concertos and symphonies remain among the most beloved and frequently performed works in the classical repertoire.

Early Life

Born into a musical family near Novgorod, Russia, Rachmaninoff entered the Moscow Conservatory at age nine. He studied under the strict discipline of Nikolai Zverev and later with Arensky and Taneyev. He graduated with the Great Gold Medal in 1892, having already composed his famous Prelude in C-sharp minor.

Career & Music

After the catastrophic premiere of his First Symphony in 1897, Rachmaninoff fell into a deep creative crisis that lasted three years. Hypnotherapy helped him recover, and his Piano Concerto No. 2 marked a glorious return. He left Russia after the 1917 Revolution and spent his remaining years in the West, concertizing relentlessly as a pianist while composing sparingly but magnificently. His enormous hands — reportedly able to span a 13th on the keyboard — became legendary.

Did you know?

Rachmaninoff's hands were so large he could span 12 piano keys with one hand. His Piano Concerto No. 2 was dedicated to Dr. Nikolai Dahl, the hypnotherapist who helped him overcome his crippling depression and compose again.

Legacy

Though critics in his lifetime sometimes dismissed his music as old-fashioned, audiences never wavered in their devotion. Rachmaninoff's Piano Concerto No. 2 has been voted the greatest piece of classical music in multiple polls. His recordings as a pianist set standards that are still studied today. He proved that Romantic expression could thrive in the modern age, and his music continues to move listeners with its passionate sincerity.
