Camille Saint-Saëns was a towering figure in French music for over six decades. A child prodigy of Mozartean proportions, he grew into a composer of supreme craftsmanship, an acclaimed organist, a brilliant pianist, and a polymath who wrote on astronomy, philosophy, and archaeology. His music combines classical clarity with Romantic warmth and dazzling orchestration.

Early Life

Born in Paris in 1835, Saint-Saëns began playing piano at age two and gave his first public recital at age ten, offering to play any Beethoven sonata from memory as an encore. He entered the Paris Conservatoire at 13 and quickly became the most admired young musician in France. Liszt called him the greatest organist in the world.

Career & Music

Saint-Saëns composed in virtually every genre — operas, symphonies, concertos, chamber music, choral works, and film scores. He was one of the first major composers to write music for cinema. His five piano concertos and three violin concertos remain staples of the repertoire. A champion of French music, he co-founded the Société Nationale de Musique to promote works by living French composers.

Did you know?

Saint-Saëns forbade public performance of The Carnival of the Animals during his lifetime, fearing the humorous work would damage his serious reputation. Only “The Swan” was allowed to be published. The full work was not heard publicly until after his death in 1921.

Legacy

Saint-Saëns bridged the gap between Berlioz and Ravel in French music. His Organ Symphony is one of the great orchestral showpieces. The Carnival of the Animals delights audiences of all ages. He was also a pioneer of film music, composing what is often considered the first original film score in 1908. His emphasis on clarity, elegance, and formal balance helped define the French musical aesthetic for generations.
