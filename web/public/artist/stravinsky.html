Igor Stravinsky is widely regarded as one of the most important and influential composers of the 20th century. His work spans an extraordinary range of styles — from the explosive primitivism of The Rite of Spring to elegant neoclassicism to late-career serialism — each phase reshaping the musical landscape.

Early Life

Born in Oranienbaum, near St. Petersburg, Russia, in 1882, Stravinsky was the son of a leading bass singer at the Mariinsky Theatre. Though his parents steered him toward law, he studied privately with Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov, who became his most important mentor and opened the door to the world of orchestration.

Career & Music

Stravinsky's career divides into three major periods. His Russian period (1907–1919) produced the three great ballets for Diaghilev's Ballets Russes. His neoclassical period (1920–1954) drew on 18th-century forms with modernist harmony. His serial period (1954–1968) explored twelve-tone techniques inspired by Webern. Throughout, his rhythmic vitality, brilliant orchestration, and structural clarity remained unmistakable.

Did you know?

The 1913 premiere of The Rite of Spring caused such an uproar that the police were called. Audience members threw vegetables, fought with each other, and the dancers could not hear the orchestra over the shouting.

Legacy

Stravinsky's influence on 20th-century music is almost impossible to overstate. The Rite of Spring alone changed the course of rhythm, harmony, and orchestration in Western music. His neoclassical works inspired generations of composers to revisit older forms, while his late serial works proved that innovation need not stop with age. He remains a towering figure in ballet, orchestral, and choral music.
