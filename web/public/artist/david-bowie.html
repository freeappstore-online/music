David Robert Jones, known as David Bowie, was one of the most influential musicians of the 20th century. A master of reinvention, he created a series of personas — Ziggy Stardust, Aladdin Sane, the Thin White Duke — that allowed him to explore different musical genres while challenging social norms around identity, gender, and sexuality.

Early Life

Born on January 8, 1947, in Brixton, London. A childhood fight permanently dilated one pupil, giving him his famous mismatched eyes. He studied art, music, and design, and played in numerous bands through the 1960s before his breakthrough. His early interest in mime, theater, and avant-garde art shaped his theatrical approach to rock music.

Career & Music

Space Oddity (1969) brought him initial fame. The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust (1972) made him a glam rock icon. He then shifted through soul (Young Americans), electronic (Low, Heroes, Lodger — the Berlin Trilogy), and dance pop (Let's Dance). His final album, Blackstar, released two days before his death, was a critically acclaimed farewell.

Did you know?

Bowie's different-colored eyes are actually a condition called anisocoria — his left pupil was permanently dilated after a childhood fight with his friend George Underwood over a girl. The two remained lifelong friends, and Underwood later designed several Bowie album covers.

Legacy

Bowie sold over 140 million records and influenced virtually every aspect of popular culture — from music to fashion to film to art. He showed that pop music could be intellectually ambitious and constantly evolving. Artists from Madonna to Lady Gaga to Radiohead cite him as a primary influence.
