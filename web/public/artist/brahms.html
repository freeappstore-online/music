Often grouped with Bach and Beethoven as one of the "Three Bs" of classical music, Brahms was a perfectionist who spent over two decades completing his first symphony. His music combines Classical structure with Romantic passion, producing works of extraordinary depth and craftsmanship.

Early Life

Born in Hamburg to a poor family, Brahms showed musical talent early and studied piano from age seven. As a teenager, he played piano in taverns and dance halls to help support his family. At twenty, he met the violinist Joseph Joachim, who introduced him to Robert and Clara Schumann — a meeting that changed his life forever.

Career & Music

Brahms settled in Vienna in 1872 and became one of the most respected composers in Europe. A staunch traditionalist, he championed absolute music against the "New German School" of Liszt and Wagner. His four symphonies, two piano concertos, violin concerto, and vast body of chamber music represent the pinnacle of Romantic-era craftsmanship within Classical forms.

Did you know?

Brahms spent 21 years completing his First Symphony, partly because he felt the weight of Beethoven's legacy. When conductor Hans von Bülow called it "Beethoven's Tenth," Brahms reportedly snapped, "Any ass can see that."

Legacy

Brahms proved that the Classical symphonic tradition could thrive in the Romantic era. His rigorous approach to form and counterpoint influenced generations of composers including Dvorák, Mahler, and Schoenberg, who called Brahms "the progressive." His four symphonies remain cornerstones of the orchestral repertoire, and his chamber music is among the richest in the entire literature.
