Samuel Cook was one of the most important figures in 20th-century American music. Often called the "King of Soul," he was a pioneer who helped create the genre by bringing gospel vocal techniques to secular pop music. His smooth, effortless tenor, impeccable phrasing, and gifted songwriting produced some of the most beautiful recordings in popular music.

Early Life

Born on January 22, 1931, in Clarksdale, Mississippi, and raised in Chicago. The son of a Baptist minister, he grew up singing gospel in his father's church. As lead singer of the Soul Stirrers from 1950 to 1956, he became the most popular gospel singer in America. His decision to cross over into secular pop music was controversial in the gospel community.

Career & Music

"You Send Me" (1957) reached No. 1 and sold 1.7 million copies. A string of hits followed — "Chain Gang," "Cupid," "Twistin' the Night Away," "Another Saturday Night." He was also a shrewd businessman — one of the first Black artists to own his own record label (SAR Records) and publishing company. "A Change Is Gonna Come" (1964), inspired by Bob Dylan's "Blowin' in the Wind," became one of the most important songs of the civil rights movement.

Did you know?

Sam Cooke's Live at the Harlem Square Club, recorded in 1963, was so raw and intense that his label refused to release it, fearing it would damage his clean-cut pop image. It wasn't released until 1985, when it was hailed as one of the greatest live albums ever recorded.

Legacy

Sam Cooke was shot and killed on December 11, 1964, at a motel in Los Angeles at age 33 under disputed circumstances. His influence on popular music is immeasurable — he proved that a Black artist could control his own business affairs, and his vocal style directly influenced Otis Redding, Marvin Gaye, Al Green, Rod Stewart, and countless others.
