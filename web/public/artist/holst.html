Gustav Holst was an English composer whose orchestral suite The Planets stands as one of the most recognizable and influential works of the 20th century. A modest, deeply private man, he drew inspiration from English folk song, Hindu literature, and astrology to create music of visionary power and startling originality.

Early Life

Born in Cheltenham, England, in 1874, Holst came from a musical family of Swedish and Latvian descent. His father was a piano teacher, and Gustav began composing as a teenager. A nerve condition in his right hand prevented him from pursuing a career as a pianist, so he turned to the trombone and composition. He studied at the Royal College of Music in London, where he formed a lifelong friendship with Ralph Vaughan Williams.

Career & Music

Holst spent most of his career as a music teacher, composing in his spare time. His interests were remarkably diverse: English folk songs, Hindu philosophy, astrology, and the music of Stravinsky and Schoenberg all fed into his work. The Planets made him famous almost overnight, but he was uncomfortable with celebrity and continued to prioritize teaching and quieter creative work. His St Paul's Suite, Egdon Heath, and choral hymns reveal a composer of far greater range than his one famous hit suggests.

Did you know?

Holst was so annoyed by the overwhelming popularity of The Planets that he once said, “Every artist ought to pray that he may not be a success.” He considered Egdon Heath, a dark orchestral tone poem inspired by Thomas Hardy, to be his finest work, yet it is rarely performed today.

Legacy

The Planets has had an immeasurable impact on popular culture. Its influence can be heard directly in John Williams' Star Wars score — “Mars, the Bringer of War” clearly inspired the Imperial March, and “Jupiter” provided a template for heroic film themes. The “Jupiter” melody was adapted as the hymn “I Vow to Thee, My Country,” sung at state occasions across Britain. Beyond The Planets, Holst's choral music, folk song arrangements, and wind band works remain central to the English musical tradition.
