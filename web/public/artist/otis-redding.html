Otis Ray Redding Jr. was the most powerful and passionate voice in 1960s soul music. His raw, emotional vocal style — a cry from the heart that could move audiences to tears — defined Southern soul and influenced every soul singer who followed. His tragically short life ended in a plane crash at just 26, three days after recording his most famous song.

Early Life

Born on September 9, 1941, in Dawson, Georgia, and raised in Macon. His father was a preacher and he grew up singing in church. He dropped out of school at 15 to support his family, working odd jobs while performing in local talent shows. He won the Macon talent show 15 consecutive weeks.

Career & Music

His first hit, "These Arms of Mine" (1962), established his raw, emotional style. He became the star of Stax Records in Memphis, recording classics like "Mr. Pitiful," "I've Been Loving You Too Long," and "Try a Little Tenderness." His performance at the Monterey Pop Festival in June 1967 introduced him to a massive white audience. Three days after recording "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay," he died in a plane crash.

Did you know?

Otis wrote "Respect" in 1965, but Aretha Franklin's 1967 version became so iconic that Otis joked onstage: "That little girl done took my song." Aretha's version became a civil rights and feminist anthem. Otis's "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay" was the first posthumous No. 1 single in US chart history.

Legacy

Otis Redding died on December 10, 1967, when his plane crashed into a lake in Wisconsin. He was 26 years old. "(Sittin' On) The Dock of the Bay," released posthumously, became the first posthumous No. 1 single in US history. His influence on soul, R&B, and rock music is incalculable — from Al Green to Bruce Springsteen to Kanye West.
