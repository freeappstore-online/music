Francis Albert Sinatra was the most popular and influential American popular music singer of the 20th century. His meticulous phrasing, emotional depth, and swinging delivery elevated pop singing from simple entertainment to a sophisticated art form. Over a career spanning six decades, he sold over 150 million records worldwide.

Early Life

Born on December 12, 1915, in Hoboken, New Jersey, to Italian immigrant parents. Inspired by Bing Crosby, he decided to become a singer. He got his break singing with the Harry James and Tommy Dorsey bands in the late 1930s. By the early 1940s, he had become the first modern teen idol — young women screamed and fainted at his concerts, a phenomenon dubbed "Sinatramania."

Career & Music

After a career slump in the early 1950s, his comeback role in From Here to Eternity (1953) won him an Academy Award. He signed with Capitol Records and, working with arranger Nelson Riddle, recorded a series of concept albums — In the Wee Small Hours, Songs for Swingin' Lovers!, Come Fly with Me — that remain the gold standard of American popular music. He later founded Reprise Records and recorded My Way and New York, New York.

Did you know?

Sinatra's version of "Fly Me to the Moon" was played by astronaut Buzz Aldrin on a portable cassette player during the Apollo 11 mission, making it the first song played on the moon. When Sinatra died, the lights on the Las Vegas Strip were dimmed in his honor.

Legacy

Sinatra won 11 Grammy Awards, an Academy Award, and the Presidential Medal of Freedom. He transformed the pop album from a collection of singles into a coherent artistic statement. His interpretations of the Great American Songbook remain definitive. He is considered the most important popular music figure of the 20th century by many music historians.
