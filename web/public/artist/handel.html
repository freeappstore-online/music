Born the same year as Bach, George Frideric Handel became one of the towering figures of the Baroque era. A cosmopolitan genius who absorbed Italian, German, and English musical traditions, he composed over 40 operas and 29 oratorios, and his Messiah remains one of the most performed and beloved works in the entire classical canon.

Early Life

Born in Halle, Germany, Handel showed musical talent early despite his father's wish that he study law. He secretly practiced on a clavichord in the attic and eventually persuaded his father to allow music lessons. After a formative period studying opera in Italy, the young composer made his way to London, where he would spend the rest of his life.

Career & Music

Handel dominated London's musical life for decades, first as an opera impresario and then as the creator of English oratorio. When Italian opera fell out of fashion in England, he reinvented himself, turning to the oratorio form and producing a string of masterworks including Messiah, Samson, and Solomon. A shrewd businessman as well as a brilliant musician, he was one of the first composers to organize and profit from public concerts.

Did you know?

Handel composed the entire Messiah — nearly three hours of music — in just 24 days. According to legend, his servant found him in tears after writing the "Hallelujah" chorus, and Handel declared, "I did think I did see all Heaven before me, and the great God Himself."

Legacy

Handel was revered in England as the greatest composer of his age, and his music has never fallen out of the repertoire. Beethoven called him "the greatest composer that ever lived." The Messiah has been performed continuously since its 1742 Dublin premiere, and the tradition of standing during the "Hallelujah" chorus persists to this day. He is buried in Westminster Abbey alongside English kings and queens.
