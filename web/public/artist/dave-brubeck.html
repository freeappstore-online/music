Dave Brubeck was a groundbreaking pianist and composer whose experiments with odd time signatures, polyrhythms, and polytonality helped expand the boundaries of jazz. His Dave Brubeck Quartet became one of the most popular jazz groups in history, and his 1959 album Time Out — featuring the iconic Take Five in 5/4 time — became one of the first jazz albums to sell over a million copies. Brubeck proved that intellectually adventurous jazz could also be enormously popular.

Early Life

Born on December 6, 1920, in Concord, California, Dave Brubeck grew up on a cattle ranch where his mother, a classically trained pianist, gave him his first music lessons. He initially studied veterinary science at the College of the Pacific before switching to music. His zoology professor reportedly told him he should be in the music conservatory instead. After serving in the Army during World War II, where he led a band in General Patton's forces, Brubeck studied composition with the French modernist Darius Milhaud at Mills College, who encouraged him to experiment with polytonality and unconventional structures.

Career & Music

Brubeck's career spanned over six decades. The Dave Brubeck Quartet, with Paul Desmond on alto saxophone, Joe Morello on drums, and Eugene Wright on bass, became one of the most popular jazz groups of the late 1950s and 1960s. Their exploration of time signatures drawn from Turkish, Indian, and African music was revolutionary. Brubeck was also a committed civil rights advocate — he canceled several lucrative concerts rather than replace his Black bassist Eugene Wright, and he refused to perform at segregated venues. Beyond jazz, he composed orchestral works, ballet scores, musicals, and a Mass, demonstrating the breadth of his musical vision.

Did you know?

Dave Brubeck nearly flunked out of music school because he couldn't read sheet music — he had learned piano entirely by ear. His professors let him graduate only after he promised never to teach, to avoid embarrassing the school.

Legacy

Dave Brubeck's legacy extends far beyond Take Five. He demonstrated that jazz could be both intellectually rigorous and hugely accessible, reaching audiences who had never listened to jazz before. His experiments with odd meters opened doors for generations of musicians. He received the National Medal of Arts, a star on the Hollywood Walk of Fame, and numerous honorary doctorates. His commitment to civil rights and racial integration in his band was ahead of its time. Brubeck showed that musical innovation, popular appeal, and social conscience could coexist in a single artist's career.
