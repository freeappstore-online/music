Robert Johnson recorded only 29 songs in his short life, but they became the foundation of modern blues and rock. His legend — selling his soul to the devil at a crossroads — is music's greatest myth.

Early Life

Born in Hazlehurst, Mississippi. Grew up in the Delta, fascinated by the blues musicians he heard at juke joints. Initially a mediocre guitarist, he disappeared for months and returned playing astonishingly well.

Career & Music

Recorded 29 songs in two sessions in 1936 and 1937, in San Antonio and Dallas. Cross Road Blues, Hellhound on My Trail, and Sweet Home Chicago became American standards.

Did you know?

Only two photographs of Robert Johnson are known to exist. His complete recordings fit on a single CD — yet they changed the course of popular music.

Legacy

Died at 27 under mysterious circumstances — likely poisoned. His recordings weren't widely heard until the 1960s, when they influenced the Rolling Stones, Eric Clapton, and Led Zeppelin. Keith Richards said: "He was like a comet that came and went."
