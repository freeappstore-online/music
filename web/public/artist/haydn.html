Joseph Haydn was the most prolific and influential composer of the Classical period. Over a career spanning five decades, he wrote 104 symphonies, 68 string quartets, and numerous operas, masses, and concertos. His wit, formal innovations, and generous spirit earned him the affectionate title "Papa Haydn" from musicians across Europe.

Early Life

Born in Rohrau, Austria, to a wheelwright and a cook, Haydn showed musical talent early and was sent to Vienna as a choirboy at St. Stephen's Cathedral at age six. After his voice broke, he spent years as a struggling freelance musician, teaching and playing, before securing his first major position.

Career & Music

Haydn spent nearly 30 years as Kapellmeister to the Esterházy family, where he had an orchestra at his disposal and the freedom to experiment. He essentially invented the string quartet and transformed the symphony from a lightweight entertainment into a serious art form. His two visits to London in the 1790s produced his greatest symphonies and made him internationally famous.

Did you know?

In the "Surprise" Symphony, Haydn placed a thundering fortissimo chord in a quiet slow movement to wake up audience members who had fallen asleep. He reportedly said, "That will make the ladies jump."

Legacy

Haydn's formal innovations laid the groundwork for everything that followed in the Classical and Romantic periods. He was Mozart's dear friend and Beethoven's teacher. His 104 symphonies and 68 string quartets remain central to the concert repertoire. His good humor, relentless invention, and mastery of musical surprise continue to delight audiences over two centuries later.
