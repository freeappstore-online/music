James Joseph Brown was the most important rhythmic innovator in 20th-century popular music. He transformed soul music into funk by shifting the emphasis from melody and harmony to rhythm and groove, creating the musical foundation for disco, hip-hop, and electronic dance music. He is the most sampled artist in music history.

Early Life

Born on May 3, 1933, in Barnwell, South Carolina, into extreme poverty. Abandoned by his mother at age four, he was raised by relatives and in a brothel run by his aunt. He shined shoes, danced for pennies, and was sent to reform school at 16 for petty theft. In prison, he formed a gospel group with Bobby Byrd, who would become his lifelong collaborator.

Career & Music

"Please, Please, Please" (1956) launched his career. Through the 1960s, his explosive live shows — the cape routine, the splits, the spins — earned him the reputation as the hardest-working man in show business. "Papa's Got a Brand New Bag" (1965) and "I Got You (I Feel Good)" (1965) redefined rhythm. "Cold Sweat" (1967) is widely considered the first funk record. Live at the Apollo (1963) is one of the greatest live albums ever recorded.

Did you know?

James Brown is the most sampled musician in history — his recordings have been sampled thousands of times. The "Funky Drummer" breakbeat alone has been used in over 1,000 songs. When he died on Christmas Day 2006, Michael Jackson was one of the last people to speak at his public memorial.

Legacy

James Brown is the most sampled musician in history — his beats and breaks form the backbone of hip-hop. He proved that rhythm could be the primary element of music. His influence extends from hip-hop to Prince to Michael Jackson, who credited Brown as his biggest inspiration. He received a Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award in 1992.
