Lead Belly was one of the most important figures in American folk and blues music. Born Huddie William Ledbetter in rural Louisiana, he possessed an encyclopedic knowledge of folk songs, blues, spirituals, cowboy songs, and work songs that he delivered with a booming voice and thundering twelve-string guitar. His recordings for the Library of Congress and commercial labels preserved dozens of songs that became standards of American music, influencing everyone from Woody Guthrie and Pete Seeger to Bob Dylan, Led Zeppelin, and Nirvana.

Early Life

Huddie William Ledbetter was born on January 20, 1888, on a plantation near Mooringsport, Louisiana, to Wesley and Sallie Ledbetter. He grew up in a musical household and was given an accordion as a child before gravitating to the guitar. By his teens he had become a formidable musician, learning from the diverse musical traditions of the rural South. In his early twenties he traveled through Texas and Louisiana, absorbing songs from field workers, prisoners, and fellow musicians. He met and was deeply influenced by Blind Lemon Jefferson, with whom he busked on the streets of Dallas around 1912.

Career & Music

Lead Belly's musical career took its most consequential turn in 1933 when folklorists John and Alan Lomax recorded him at Angola prison for the Library of Congress. After his release in 1934, he moved to New York and became a central figure in the folk music scene, performing at hootenannies and left-wing gatherings alongside Woody Guthrie, Pete Seeger, and the Almanac Singers. His repertoire was staggering in its breadth -- hundreds of songs spanning blues, folk ballads, spirituals, children's songs, cowboy tunes, and prison work songs. He recorded extensively for the Library of Congress, Musicraft, RCA Victor, Capitol, and Folkways Records. His performances were legendary for their raw power, his booming voice and driving twelve-string guitar filling rooms without amplification.

Did you know?

Lead Belly's "Goodnight, Irene" became a massive No. 1 hit in 1950 -- but only after his death. The Weavers, featuring Pete Seeger, recorded a version that spent 13 weeks atop the Billboard charts and sold over two million copies. Lead Belly himself never saw a dime from it, having died just six months before its release.

Legacy

Lead Belly's influence on American and world music is immeasurable. His songs have been recorded by hundreds of artists across every genre: the Weavers took "Goodnight, Irene" to No. 1, Lonnie Donegan launched the British skiffle craze with "Rock Island Line," Creedence Clearwater Revival covered "Cotton Fields" and "Midnight Special," and Nirvana famously closed their MTV Unplugged set with "Where Did You Sleep Last Night." He was inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 1988 as an early influence, and into the Louisiana Music Hall of Fame. His Library of Congress recordings remain an invaluable archive of American folk tradition. More than any other single artist, Lead Belly served as a bridge between the rural folk music of the 19th century and the popular music of the 20th.
