Talking Heads emerged from the New York punk scene at CBGB but quickly evolved into something far stranger and more ambitious. David Byrne's nervous, jerky vocals and oblique lyrics, combined with Tina Weymouth's funky bass, Chris Frantz's precise drumming, and Jerry Harrison's textured keyboards, created a unique fusion of punk, funk, world music, and art-pop.

Early Life

Formed in 1975 by Rhode Island School of Design students David Byrne, Chris Frantz, and Tina Weymouth. They began playing at CBGB alongside the Ramones, Television, and Blondie. Jerry Harrison (formerly of Jonathan Richman's Modern Lovers) joined in 1977.

Career & Music

Talking Heads: 77 introduced Psycho Killer. More Songs About Buildings and Food (1978) began their collaboration with Brian Eno. Fear of Music (1979) and Remain in Light (1980) incorporated African polyrhythms and Afrobeat influences. Stop Making Sense (1984) became the greatest concert film ever made.

Did you know?

For Stop Making Sense, David Byrne wore an enormous oversized suit — inspired by Japanese Noh theater and Kabuki — that became one of the most iconic images in music film. Director Jonathan Demme filmed the show so brilliantly that Roger Ebert called it "the greatest concert film ever made."

Legacy

They disbanded in 1991 amid tensions between Byrne and the other members. Stop Making Sense remains the gold standard for concert films. Their fusion of punk energy, funk grooves, and intellectual ambition paved the way for alternative rock, indie music, and art-pop.
