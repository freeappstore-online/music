Franz Schubert lived just 31 years, yet he produced one of the most astonishing bodies of work in musical history — over 600 songs, nine symphonies, chamber music of unparalleled beauty, and piano works of deep emotional power. He bridged the Classical elegance of Mozart and Beethoven with the emotional intensity of Romanticism.

Early Life

Born in Vienna to a schoolmaster father, Schubert learned violin, piano, and singing as a child. At age eleven, he became a choirboy at the Imperial Court Chapel and studied at the Stadtkonvikt school, where Antonio Salieri recognized his exceptional talent. By seventeen, he had already composed his first masterpiece — the song "Gretchen am Spinnrade."

Career & Music

Unlike most composers of his era, Schubert never held a major musical post. He lived among a devoted circle of friends — the famous "Schubertiades" — informal gatherings where his songs and chamber works were performed. Though he struggled financially and never achieved wide fame in his lifetime, he composed at a breathtaking pace, transforming the art song into a vehicle of profound emotional expression.

Did you know?

In 1815 alone, the 18-year-old Schubert composed 144 songs — sometimes two or three in a single day. He reportedly wrote Erlkönig, one of the greatest songs ever composed, in a single feverish sitting.

Legacy

Schubert died at 31, buried near Beethoven at his own request. Much of his music was unpublished during his lifetime — the "Great" C major Symphony was not performed until Schumann discovered the manuscript a decade after Schubert's death. Today he is recognized as the father of the art song and one of the most naturally gifted melodists in the history of Western music.
